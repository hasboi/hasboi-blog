Mosaic of Everyone You've Ever Loved

30 April 2025

You ever catch yourself doing something small--something theirs--and realize you've forgotten where they end and you begin?

Like yesterday, when I rinsed my mouth twice without thinking. Just like she taught me. We haven't spoken in years. Haven't touched in longer.

But there she was--in the way I tilted my head at the mirror, in the exact rhythm of my spit hitting porcelain. Muscle memory written in someone else's handwriting.

So that's when I realized:

I'm not one person. I'm a scrapbook.
A living museum of every "I love you" that ever took root in me.

--I still chew straws with my teeth, just like my high school crush. She kisses someone else now. My teeth remembers.
--I hum when I'm scared, same off-key tune Mom used to sing washing dishes. I used to think it was annoying. But now it's mine.
--I cut sandwiches diagonally because a boy with skinned knees once swore triangles tasted better. He was right. I still eat like we're twelve. And he's married now.

The way I stretch out "duuuude" like taffy--that's not me.
That's the ghost of a boy from sophomore who promised we'd be friends forever before moving to Canada and forgetting my name.
His voice lives in my throat now.

Funny, how love lingers like a phantom limb.

Not just the epic romances. The tiny intimacies:
The "text me when you're safe" that stopped getting replies.
The "we should do this again" that never happened.
The sunsets shared through train windows, gold and temporary, already fading as you tried to memorize them.

They never really leave. They just sink deeper.
Become part of your foundation.
Become part of your architecture.

You think you've outgrown them until--
--a song, a scent, a stupid inside joke--
and suddenly you're a Matryoshka doll of ghosts,
each nested inside the other,
still whispering.

Love doesn't vanish.
It migrates.
From their hands to yours,
from their mouth to your memory,
from their heartbeat to your bones.

So go ahead.
Miss them.
Keep them.
Let them live in the way you stir your tea,
in the nicknames you can't retire,
in the quiet moments when you swear you can still hear them laughing.

Because the people who matter never really leave.
They just learn how to live inside you.
